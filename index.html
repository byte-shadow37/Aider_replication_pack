Abstract

Code clones, or duplicated code parts, make software harder to maintain, read, and keep consistent. Finding and fixing code clones is important for keeping software quality high. Recently, improve- ments in Artificial Intelligence (AI), especially Large Language Models (LLMs), have created new ways to improve how we name methods during refactoring. Building on these improvements, our paper looks at Aider, a command-line AI assistant that uses LLMs to suggest method names during refactoring. Our experiments tested Aider’s ability to suggest good method names on the JHotDraw project using seven LLMs: Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-3.5, GPT-4o, GPT-5, DeepSeek-R1, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, and Claude Opus 4.1. We tested two ways: (1) comparing single method names from refactoring, and (2) asking each model to suggest 10 names for each method. The results show that different models perform differently at naming tasks. GPT-4o did the best, getting the highest similarity scores in 7 out of 20 cases for single names. GPT-5 also did well, getting the highest scores in 5 cases when comparing all 10 names. DeepSeek- R1 got the highest scores in 6 cases. Claude Opus 4.1 was especially good at naming UI and layout methods, with the highest scores in 2 cases. Gemini 2.5 Pro had mixed results with highest scores in 4 cases. Claude 3.7 Sonnet created good names but never got the highest score. GPT-3.5 had the most problems, often creating placeholder names with "temp" in them. All models followed Java’s camelCase naming style. No models used PascalCase, snake_case, or kebab-case. GPT-4o, GPT-5, and DeepSeek-R1 were the best at creating names that make sense and follow naming rules. Overall, Aider with advanced LLMs gives developers a choice to use LLMs for suggesting names for extracted methods.
